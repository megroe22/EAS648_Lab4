Now lets assess the sentiment of the different song lines in the song of the three songs we chose to look at. We apply a zero to the cases without a negative or positive word. Finally, we create a new variable that calculates the total sentiment (positive count minus negative).

song_sentiment <- tidy_song %>%
  inner_join(get_sentiments("bing")) %>%
  count(track_title, index = line, sentiment) %>%
  spread(sentiment, n, fill = 0) %>%
  mutate(sentiment = positive - negative)

In 2023, Tesla earned a whopping $1.79 billion from selling carbon credits. Since 2009, Tesla has earned over $7 billion. Since 2009, this revenue stream has netted Tesla over $9 billion, making it a cornerstone of the company's profitability. Carbon credits are essentially permits that allow companies to emit a certain amount of carbon dioxide. Companies that produce fewer emissions can sell their extra credit to those that have exceeded their limits. Tesla, with its zero emission vehicles, generates a lot of these credits and sells them to traditional automakers who need to meet regulatory requirements. But here's the catch. By selling these carbon credits, Tesla is effectively allowing other companies to continue polluting. Instead of reducing their own emissions, these companies just buy their way out of trouble, undermining the overall goal of reducing that carbon footprint. In an interview with Dr. Emily Clark, an environmental policy expert, she explains Tesla's significant revenue from carbon credits highlights a problematic reliance on these markets. While Tesla's cars are indeed less polluting, the sale of carbon credits to less green automakers allows those companies to delay making their own necessary changes for the environment. Tesla's ability to generate and sell carbon credits has turned it into a financial juggernaut, but the strategy is a double-edged sword. Other automakers buy these credits instead of investing themselves in sustainable practices, perpetuating a cycle of dependency on outdated, polluting practices. This practice of selling indulgences in the form of carbon credits allows Tesla to profit while contributing to a system that does little to combat the current issues of climate change. While Tesla enjoys its green reputation and financial success, the true cost is borne by the environment. Carbon credits provide a convenient smokescreen, allowing businesses to claim green credentials without making any meaningful changes. Supporting the mission of saving our planet is important, but we need to demand transparency and real action from these companies. True sustainability requires more than just buying and selling these carbon credits. It demands genuine, actionable reductions in emissions because the future of our planet absolutely depends on it.
